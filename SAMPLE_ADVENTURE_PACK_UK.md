# Sample Adventure Pack: United Kingdom

A complete walk-through of the UK Adventure Pack, mirroring the Brazil sample so explainer videos and marketing copy can lean on whichever feels closer to the audience. The UK pack uses the same 12-mission shape as every other live country pack on the site (46 and counting), with content hand-written for British family travel and the four-nation reality of England, Scotland, Wales and Northern Ireland.

## What an Adventure Pack is

A country-specific mission set for families travelling that country (or worldschooling about it). Same 12 missions in the same order in every pack, with country-specific content in each. Lives at a URL like `/adventure-packs/united-kingdom`. A parent reads it themselves, or hands the phone to a child via their personal Adventure Passport QR code; completed missions fire stamps into the passport.

## The 12 missions, using the UK as the example

### 1. Map mission 🗺️

A handful of country-orienting questions:

- Which continent is the UK on? *Europe, just off the north-west coast.*
- What four countries make up the United Kingdom? *England, Scotland, Wales and Northern Ireland.*
- What is the capital of the UK? *London. Also the capital of England. Edinburgh is Scottish, Cardiff Welsh, Belfast Northern Irish.*
- What is the highest mountain in the UK? (older kids) *Ben Nevis in Scotland, 1,345 metres. Tiny compared to the Himalayas, but plenty steep enough.*
- Why do British people say "Great Britain" sometimes and "United Kingdom" other times? (older kids) *Great Britain is the big island that includes England, Scotland and Wales. The United Kingdom adds Northern Ireland. Both terms get used to mean roughly the same thing, but they aren't identical.*

Earns: 🗺️ **Map Reader** stamp.

### 2. Language 🗣️

English is the headline language, so this section pivots to the regional vocabulary that actually feels British. Eight to attempt in the wild:

- Hello (north of England, casual) — *Hiya / alright?*
- Thanks (very British) — *Cheers*
- Cup of tea — *A cuppa*
- Umbrella — *Brolly*
- Hug (Welsh) — *Cwtch* (COOTCH)
- Small (Scottish) — *Wee*
- Cheeky / playful (older kids) — *Cheeky*
- Child (Scottish, older kids) — *Bairn*

Earns: 🗣️ **Local Lingo** stamp after 3+ phrases attempted.

### 3. Money 💰

- Currency: Pound Sterling (GBP, £)
- Recommended budget: £20
- Challenge: figure out how to spend it on something local in a day.

Earns: 💰 **Money Changer** stamp.

### 4. Food 🍜

Six dishes worth tracking down, picked for the British family-table classics that travel well to a child's plate:

- 🐟 **Fish and chips**: white fish (cod or haddock) deep-fried in crispy batter, served with thick-cut chips, mushy peas, salt and vinegar. Best eaten out of paper at the seaside.
- 🥩 **Sunday roast**: roast meat (beef, lamb, chicken, pork), Yorkshire puddings, roast potatoes, vegetables, stuffing and gravy. Sunday lunchtime, ideally in a pub by a fire.
- 🍳 **Full English breakfast**: bacon, sausage, baked beans, mushrooms, grilled tomato, fried egg, black pudding, hash brown, toast. Often called a "fry-up". Glorious, ridiculous, a cure for everything.
- 🥧 **Cornish pasty**: folded shortcrust pastry filled with beef, potato, onion and swede. Originally a portable lunch for Cornish tin miners, who'd hold it by the thick crimped crust along the edge.
- 🍞 **Yorkshire pudding**: a puffy, hollow, golden bowl of batter, baked in hot fat until it rises like a balloon. Eaten with roast beef and gravy. Northerners take this very seriously.
- 🍰 **Scone with clotted cream and jam**: a soft round cake split open and loaded with thick clotted cream and strawberry jam. In Devon the cream goes on first then jam. In Cornwall, jam first then cream. People argue about this.

Earns: 🍜 **Brave Eater** stamp after 3+ foods tried.

### 5. Geography 🌍

Six places worth knowing beyond London:

- 🏙️ **London**: Big Ben, the Tower of London, the Tube, royal parks, museums, and over 300 languages spoken in the streets.
- 🏔️ **Scottish Highlands**: wild mountains, deep lochs (including Loch Ness), bagpipes, kilts, shaggy Highland cattle.
- 🌊 **Welsh coast**: 1,400km of coast with castles every few miles. Wales is the only nation with an 870-mile path that walks all the way around the whole country.
- ⛰️ **Lake District**: in the north-west of England. Steep stone-walled hills, deep blue lakes, Wordsworth and Beatrix Potter country.
- 🏖️ **Cornwall**: the far south-west of England, sticking out into the Atlantic. Surfing beaches, fishing villages, Cornish pasties, the warmest weather in the UK.
- 🪨 **Giant's Causeway**: on the north Northern Irish coast. 40,000 hexagonal columns of basalt rock stepping into the sea. Made by an ancient volcano (or, the legends say, by a giant called Finn McCool).

Earns: 🌍 **Geography Genius** stamp.

### 6. Scavenger hunt 🔎

A list of distinctly-British things to spot in real life:

- A red telephone box (rarer than they were, but villages still have them)
- A black London taxi cab
- A bright red postbox with the royal cypher on it (E II R or C III R)
- A traditional pub with a wooden hanging sign painted with a coat of arms or animal
- A field full of sheep (the UK has more sheep than people in some areas)
- A small stone village church with a graveyard around it
- A castle, or the ruins of one (the UK has thousands)
- A cottage with a thatched (straw) roof
- A robin in a hedge
- A bilingual Welsh / English road sign (older kids, in Wales)
- A blue plaque on a building marking where someone famous lived (older kids)
- A piece of an old Roman wall or a Roman mosaic (older kids — England has loads)

Earns: 🔎 **Scavenger Hunter** stamp.

### 7. Animal spotter 🐾

Seven animals to look for, with where to actually find them:

- 🦊 **Urban fox**: in gardens, dustbins, suburban streets at night. The most British wild animal.
- 🐦‍⬛ **Common magpie**: "One for sorrow, two for joy" — black-and-white birds you greet for luck.
- 🐿️ **Grey squirrel**: in every park. Cheeky, fast, will steal your sandwich.
- 🐑 **Sheep in a field**: more sheep than people in Wales and parts of Scotland.
- 🦌 **Red or roe deer**: roe deer slip across country roads at dawn. Red deer in Scottish glens.
- 🐿️ **Red squirrel** (older kids): rare now, mostly in Scotland and Anglesey. Tufty ears, ginger fur.
- 🦔 **Hedgehog** (older kids): snuffling along a hedge at dusk. Numbers have crashed, celebrate every one.

Earns: 🐾 **Animal Spotter** stamp after 3+ animals spotted.

### 8. Senses ✨

Five prompts for the kid to write down what they actually noticed:

- **Smell**: Rain on warm tarmac, fresh-cut grass, woodsmoke from pub fires, sausages cooking in a B&B at 8am, salty sea air, sheep in a hill field, jasmine on a summer hedge.
- **Hear**: "Alright?" as a greeting, seagulls over a chip shop, an accent that changes every 30 miles, sheep across a valley, church bells on a Sunday, a kettle whistling.
- **Taste**: Salty chips with vinegar, hot tea with milk, strawberry jam on warm scones, mint sauce with lamb, brown sauce on a fry-up, the buttery flake of a Cornish pasty.
- **Feel**: Wind on a cliff edge, soft Welsh rain, cold North Sea water on bare feet, the weight of a wool jumper, mud on your boots, a wool blanket on a chilly evening.
- **See**: What's the most incredible thing right in front of you right now? Open prompt for the kid to write.

Earns: ✨ **Sense Seeker** stamp.

### 9. Stories 📖

Five real UK stories, each with a question, a fact-rich answer, and (for some) a thinking question to chew on together:

- **Does the Loch Ness Monster actually exist?** Loch Ness holds more freshwater than every lake in England and Wales combined. Locals have told stories of a monster for 1,500 years. The modern legend really kicked off in 1933. The most famous photo was later confessed as a hoax (model neck on a toy submarine). Scientists with sonar found nothing big enough. People still want her to be real. *Thinking: why do people still want to believe even when scientists say there's nothing there?*
- **Who built Stonehenge, and why?** 30 enormous stones in a circle on a windy plain. The biggest are 4m tall and 25 tonnes. They've been there for 5,000 years, older than the pyramids. The bluestones were dragged 250km from Wales using ropes and human muscle. Leading theories: a giant calendar (the stones line up with sunrise on the longest and shortest days), a temple to the sun, a burial place, or all three at once. *Thinking: if you had to build one thing that would stand for 5,000 years, what would you want it to do for whoever found it?*
- **Why do Brits drive on the left?** About a third of the world drives on the left, mostly ex-British Empire (Australia, India, South Africa, Japan-ish). The leading theory: in the days of swords, most people were right-handed, so riders kept their sword arm closest to oncoming strangers. Riding on the left also lets you mount your horse onto the road safely. Napoleon (left-handed) preferred the right; as his armies marched across Europe, most countries he conquered switched to the right. Britain, which Napoleon never beat, kept driving on the left, and so did most of its empire.
- **What was the Blitz?** (older kids) Between September 1940 and May 1941, Nazi Germany bombed London almost every single night for over 8 months. Around 40,000 civilians died across the UK. Around 1.4 million Londoners lost their homes. People slept in Tube stations turned into shelters. Children were evacuated to the countryside, often to families they'd never met. Britain did not surrender. You can still find buildings in London with bomb damage left visible as a memorial. *Thinking: where do you think that kind of resilience comes from?*
- **Why did the modern world begin in Britain?** (older kids) Between roughly 1760 and 1840, in a small island in the North Atlantic, the Industrial Revolution happened. People stopped making cloth by hand and started making it in steam-powered factories. Coal mines fed huge furnaces that produced iron. Cities like Manchester, Birmingham and Glasgow exploded. Britain became the richest country in the world (powered partly by its empire and, for a long time, by the slave trade, which the country eventually banned). The cost was enormous: terrible working conditions, child labour, smoke-choked cities, polluted rivers. Almost every modern factory, train station and city in the world traces back to ideas first tried 250 years ago in northern England.

Earns: 📖 **Story Keeper** stamp.

## Why the UK pack works for British families specifically

Three things about this pack that are different from a "going abroad" pack like Brazil.

- **The kids might be doing it at home, not on holiday.** It's just as legitimate to do the UK pack on a school holiday in Cornwall or a weekend visiting grandparents in Scotland as it is to do Brazil on a five-month trip. The home country still counts as a country worth noticing.
- **Four-nation accuracy matters.** The pack quietly insists that Scotland, Wales and Northern Ireland are not "England with accents". Place names, foods, words, animals, stories are spread across all four nations on purpose.
- **The Brave Eater stamp is sneakier here.** When a kid says "I've eaten fish and chips a thousand times, that doesn't count", the answer is: try the Cornish pasty, the Sunday roast Yorkshire pudding, the proper scone with clotted cream and jam in the right order. The food list is built for that argument.
