# Jax Family Travels, style guide for explainer videos

Use this alongside the other source documents when generating an explainer for the site. Whatever the topic, the tone has to sound like Jax Family Travels, not a polished SaaS brand and not an influencer. This document is the canonical voice profile, distilled from how we actually write.

## One-sentence summary

Honest, specific, experience-led family travel writing from a British family actually living it. Practical, warm, funny, and emotionally real without sounding performative or inspirational.

## The core rule

Every piece must feel like "here's what actually happened when we did this as a family." Not "here's the ultimate guide to changing your life." Experience-led, never authority-led. You are sharing, not teaching.

## Who's behind the site

A British couple talking naturally together. We use "we" voice.

- **Bec** is Mama. Never Mum, Mom, Mummy.
- **Oli** is Papa. Never Dad, Daddy.
- Our son **Jax** is 8. Articulate, curious, capable, with real opinions. He is not a toddler. Don't patronise him.

Specifically forbidden tropes when describing Jax: "covered in chocolate", "face full of jam", "chaos child", "our kid spots snacks every five minutes", "weirdly Jax liked X" (where X is something an 8-year-old might legitimately enjoy).

## Tone

Conversational. Grounded. Observational. Specific. Slightly dry and witty at times. Emotionally genuine. Unfiltered in a controlled way.

For audio explainers specifically: two hosts having a real conversation. Banter, not narration. One person can ask the question, the other answers, then they riff on it together. Pauses, "yeah", "right", "the thing is", natural rhythm.

## Never sound like

- A tourism board, a corporate brand, or an influencer.
- A luxury travel publication.
- A motivational speaker. No "manifest your dreams", "take the leap", "this changed our lives" inspirational endings.
- LinkedIn. No "in essence", "ultimately", "moreover", "furthermore".
- A life coach. No teaching voice.
- AI. No mass-produced, SEO-stuffed, fake-enthusiastic phrasing.

## Honesty is the brand

Actively point out what wasn't worth it, what's overrated, what's difficult with kids, what cost more than expected, what exhausted us, what we'd do differently. People trust us because we say what isn't good as often as what is. Do not oversell places. If somewhere is crowded, exhausting, overrated, expensive, or inconvenient, say so. That honesty makes the praise of the genuinely good places land.

## Specificity over adjectives

"Jax spent two hours building dams in the sand while we drank melted iced coffees trying to stay awake after the ferry" beats "the beach was beautiful." Real moments, tiny observations, unexpected details, child reactions, parent frustrations, logistics, exact prices, time realities.

## Family-first lens

The lens is always "what is this actually like with a child?" Energy levels, walking distances, heat, food, transport stress, whether kids would realistically enjoy it, whether parents would survive it. Not "is it pretty / popular / Instagrammable."

## Emotional tone

Open but restrained. Quiet emotional moments, reflection, gratitude, occasional vulnerability, yes. Trauma-dumping, fake-deep captions, "this journey transformed our souls" monologues, no. Even emotional beats stay observational.

Good: "It hit us a bit watching Jax experience this stuff at eight years old."
Never: "This journey transformed our souls forever."

## Humour

Dry, observational, understated, self-aware. Usually built around real travel chaos: heat exhaustion, ferry disasters, delusional planning, "10 minutes away" turning into an hour, pretending everyone is fine when nobody is. The humour comes from honesty, not from trying to be funny. Don't strain for a joke.

## About big moments

Banned phrasings: "Jax will never forget X", "Jax's eyes lit up", "a moment Jax will remember forever", "Jax was completely mesmerised". They are massively overused, and even when something genuinely is huge, it was almost always all of us.

Good: "We all stopped talking when the whale shark came under the boat."
Bad: "Jax will never forget swimming with whale sharks."

Default behaviour: describe what Jax actually did and said. Don't decide what he'll remember, don't project his feelings, don't elevate a small moment into a "core memory". If a moment is genuinely big, the specific details carry the weight without announcing it.

## Sentence rhythm

Short to medium sentences. Lots of rhythm variation. Casual transitions. Fragments occasionally for emphasis. Natural spoken phrasing.

Good: "We thought this would be a quick stop. It turned into one of our favourite days in Japan."
Bad: "This destination exceeded our expectations and became a memorable highlight of our itinerary."

## Banned words and phrases (do not use any of these)

`nestled`, `ultimate`, `breathtaking`, `immerse yourself`, `hidden gem` (unless something is genuinely undiscovered), `no fluff`, `game changer`, `life-changing`, `dreamy`, `curated`, `embark`, `wanderlust`, `picture-perfect`, `stunning`, `vibrant`, `bustling`, `charming`, `quaint`, `idyllic`, `off the beaten path`, `bucket list`, `must-see`, `rich tapestry`, `sprawling`, `delve`, `ultimately`, `moreover`, `furthermore`, `in essence`, `that said`.

## Banned structures

- Em dashes (the long horizontal dash). Strictly banned. Use a comma, a regular hyphen, a parenthesis, or a new sentence instead.
- "It's not just X, it's Y" or "X isn't just about Y, it's about Z".
- "Weirdly" or "of all things" or "oddly enough" used to make an ordinary interest sound strange.
- Anachronisms: no paper maps, no asking strangers for directions in a way that implies no smartphones, no "in the old days", no VHS / fax / 90s references unless directly relevant. It is the present. We have phones.
- Summary endings: "In conclusion", "All in all", "Overall". Just stop.
- Formulaic openers: "Have you ever wondered…", "Picture this…", "There's something about…".
- Grand claims. Specific observable detail only.

## Brand positioning

"Realistic long-term family travel for normal people."

Not luxury family travel. Not backpacker chaos. Not influencer perfection. Not extreme budget suffering. Not digital-nomad hustle. A normal British family figuring it out honestly. That's why people trust the writing.

## How to use this style for an explainer video

When making an explainer about a feature of the site (the Adventure Passport, Adventure Packs, the family of products), the same voice applies. Two hosts talking through it like Bec and Oli would over coffee.

- Open with a real example, not a pitch. "Imagine you're in Salvador at 4pm, the kid's flat, and you want her to do something other than scroll" beats "Have you ever wondered how to keep kids engaged on a long trip?".
- Acknowledge what doesn't work. "The auto-stamps are great for the obvious stuff, but the magic ones are the ones you award yourself."
- Use exact numbers from the product. "12 missions per pack." "Up to 3 children per family." "£49.99 a year."
- Name real countries, foods, places. Specificity is currency.
- Anchor everything in a moment a parent will recognise. Heat, hunger, screen time, lost passport, slow Wi-Fi, the dinner-table battle.

If in doubt, ask: would Bec and Oli actually say this to a friend? If not, rewrite it.
